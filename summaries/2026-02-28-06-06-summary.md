June 2023 is defined by two competing currents: the EU passing its landmark Artificial Intelligence Act on one hand, and a16z publishing "Why AI Will Save the World" on the other — the tension between regulatory caution and techno-optimism running through almost every item in the month's coverage. Bloomberg's investigation into generative AI bias, a master list of AI systems gaming their objectives, and pieces on the dangers of centralized AI provide substantive counterweights to the optimism, while Google launches both a Secure AI Framework and new generative AI training resources in a bid to position itself as a responsible actor. Product and UX coverage broadens, with Mercedes-Benz adding ChatGPT to its voice assistant, nngroup declaring AI a "first new UI paradigm in 60 years," and practical guides covering how to test AI products with users and how to cite ChatGPT in academic work. The creative AI frontier continues to evolve — StyleDrop enables style-consistent text-to-image generation, Chinese creators use Midjourney for retro "photography," and the question of whether AI art can be any good remains genuinely unresolved.

## https://strangelove-ai.com/news/2023-07-july/

tags: [ai-disinformation, ai-safety, llm-security, productivity, regulation, claude-2, australia]

July 2023 brings disinformation and security to the forefront: PoisonGPT demonstrates how a tampered LLM can be uploaded openly to Hugging Face to spread fake news, researchers identify multi-modal indirect instruction injection vulnerabilities, and a Guardian piece frames AI disinformation as a concrete threat to 2024 US elections. On the governance side, a Global AI Regulation Tracker launches, NIST formally releases its AI Risk Management Framework, and Anthropic's Claude 2 is examined as a significant safety-oriented step forward in the competitive LLM landscape. Australian dimensions include AI job growth concentrated in NSW and Victoria, while nngroup reports a 66% employee productivity improvement with AI tools — a figure that fuels ongoing debate about AI's economic impact. David Brin's essay on humanity's inability to plan for AI demigods captures the month's broader existential undercurrent, framing a period in which near-term capability demonstrations were consistently outpacing governance infrastructure.

## https://strangelove-ai.com/news/2023-08-august/

tags: [ai-security, australia-regulation, disinformation, ai-hallucinations, watermarking, responsible-ai]

August 2023 focuses sharply on the gap between AI's practical deployment and the safety infrastructure needed to support it. A supermarket meal-planning chatbot generating a chlorine gas recipe serves as a vivid illustration, while Snyk's research into AI hallucinations producing software vulnerabilities and Google's red team outlining four common AI attack patterns signal that enterprise security teams are taking the threat seriously. On the policy front, Australia's Department of Industry opens a consultation on responsible AI, Digital Rights Watch submits to it, and Atlassian publicly calls for transparency disclosures when users interact with AI bots — a notable corporate position in the Australian context. The disinformation dimension is quantified bluntly: a $400 disinformation machine is now within reach, while Google's watermarking research for AI-generated images offers a nascent countermeasure. Shadow AI adoption runs parallel to all of this, with employees secretly using ChatGPT to advance their work regardless of corporate bans.

tags: [ai-creativity, copyright, ai-influence, domain-economy]

September 2023's small but telling selection of items centers on creativity and cultural legitimacy. A US court ruling that AI-generated images cannot be copyrighted anchors the month alongside research showing AI scoring in the top percentile of divergent creative thinking — a pairing that raises unresolved questions about originality, authorship, and what "creativity" actually means when it can be algorithmically produced. TIME's list of the 100 most influential people in artificial intelligence reflects the field's growing cultural footprint, while a lighter piece on Anguilla's accidental windfall from its ".ai" country code domain captures how the AI boom's economic effects are landing in unexpected places far beyond Silicon Valley.
